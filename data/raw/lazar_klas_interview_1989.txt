KLAS-TV EYEWITNESS NEWS — SPECIAL REPORT
"UFOs: The Best Evidence"
Aired: November 10, 1989
Interviewer: George Knapp, Investigative Reporter, KLAS-TV Las Vegas
Subject: Robert Scott Lazar

[TRANSCRIPT BEGINS]

KNAPP: Tonight, a Las Vegas scientist makes an extraordinary claim. He says he worked on alien spacecraft at a secret base near Area 51. Bob Lazar, welcome.

LAZAR: Thank you, George.

KNAPP: Let's start from the beginning. How did you end up working out there?

LAZAR: In late 1988, I was hired to work at a facility known as S4, which is located approximately 15 miles south of Groom Lake, at the base of the Papoose Mountain range. I was recruited after meeting Edward Teller at a lecture at Los Alamos. He made a phone call, and within a few weeks I was contacted by EG&G and brought out to the Nevada Test Site.

KNAPP: What was your role at S4?

LAZAR: I was brought on as a senior staff physicist. My specific assignment was to reverse-engineer the propulsion system of one of nine flying discs that were stored in hangars built into the mountainside at Papoose Lake. The craft I worked on was referred to as the "Sport Model" due to its small, sleek design.

KNAPP: Nine discs? And you're saying these were extraterrestrial craft?

LAZAR: That was the briefing I was given, yes. I was shown documents — briefing documents — that stated the craft were of extraterrestrial origin, recovered from archaeological digs and from crash retrievals. The technology was clearly not human-made. The manufacturing had no visible seams, no weld marks, no fasteners. The interior was scaled for beings approximately three to four feet in height.

KNAPP: Tell us about the propulsion system.

LAZAR: The propulsion system uses Element 115 as its fuel source. This is an element that does not exist naturally on Earth — at least it didn't at the time. Element 115 is a super-heavy element. When it's bombarded with protons in a small particle accelerator within the craft, it transmutes to Element 116, which immediately decays and releases antimatter. The antimatter is then reacted with matter in an annihilation reaction, and the resultant energy is converted by a thermoelectric generator into electrical energy. But the key is that Element 115, when proton-bombarded, also produces its own gravitational field — a gravity wave. The amplifiers in the craft can amplify this gravity wave and focus it, allowing the craft to distort space-time and essentially pull its destination toward it.

KNAPP: This sounds like science fiction, Bob.

LAZAR: I understand that. But I held Element 115 in my hand. It's a stable isotope — an orange-colored, heavy metallic disc about the size of a half-dollar. The supply at S4 consisted of approximately 500 pounds of machined Element 115 wedges. I was told the element was not synthesizable with human technology and that it was provided along with the craft.

KNAPP: When exactly did you start there?

LAZAR: I first went out to S4 in December 1988. I worked there on a limited basis — I would be flown out in the evening and brought back before dawn. My employment lasted until approximately March or April of 1989, when my security clearance was revoked after I brought friends out to the perimeter to watch test flights.

KNAPP: You brought people out to watch?

LAZAR: I did. On several Wednesday evenings in March 1989, I brought friends — including John Lear and Gene Huff — to a public vantage point on Highway 375, near the boundary of the restricted area. We observed and videotaped a bright, luminous object performing maneuvers that are impossible for any known aircraft — sudden stops, right-angle turns at high speed, and instantaneous acceleration.

KNAPP: Have you been threatened?

LAZAR: Yes. After I was terminated, I was followed. My phone was tapped. I was told in no uncertain terms that if I discussed what I had seen, there would be consequences. That's why I initially came forward anonymously. I felt the public had a right to know, and I also felt that going public was actually the safest thing I could do — it's harder to silence someone once they've already talked.

[TRANSCRIPT ENDS]
