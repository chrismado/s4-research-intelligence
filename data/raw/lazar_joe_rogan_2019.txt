THE JOE ROGAN EXPERIENCE — Episode #1315
Recorded: June 20, 2019
Host: Joe Rogan
Guest: Bob Lazar, accompanied by filmmaker Jeremy Corbell

[TRANSCRIPT EXCERPT — Key Segments]

ROGAN: So Bob, thirty years later, here we are. You first came forward in 1989. Has your story changed at all?

LAZAR: No. The core of what I experienced hasn't changed because it's what happened. I worked at a facility called S4, south of Area 51, near Papoose Lake. I was hired in late 1988 to work on reverse-engineering an extraterrestrial propulsion system. That's what I saw, that's what I worked on.

ROGAN: And you still maintain that you held Element 115 in your hands?

LAZAR: Absolutely. It was a stable form of Element 115 — heavy, metallic, orange in color. When you machine it and bombard it with protons, it generates its own gravitational field. That's how the propulsion system worked. The energy release from the antimatter reaction powered the craft, but the gravity amplification is what allowed it to manipulate space-time.

ROGAN: Now, Element 115 was actually synthesized in 2003 at a lab in Russia. They called it Moscovium. But it was wildly unstable — lasted for fractions of a second.

LAZAR: Right. What they synthesized is not the same isotope. The isotope at S4 was a stable form. Just because you can create an unstable version of an element doesn't mean a stable isotope can't exist. Different isotopes of the same element can have radically different properties. Think of carbon-12 versus carbon-14.

ROGAN: Let's talk about your background. You said you attended MIT and Caltech. People have looked into this and can't find records.

LAZAR: I know. And that's one of the most frustrating things. I did attend MIT for a period — not a full degree program, but coursework. I also took courses at Caltech. When I came forward, my records were scrubbed. My employment history at Los Alamos was denied initially too, until a phone directory listing was found with my name in it. That's the kind of thing you're dealing with — active erasure.

CORBELL: I want to point out that Bob's name was indeed found in the Los Alamos National Laboratory phone directory. That's a verified fact. Whatever happened to his academic records, his presence at Los Alamos is documented.

ROGAN: What about the polygraph tests?

LAZAR: I took multiple polygraph examinations. The results indicated I was being truthful. Terry Tavernetti administered one of them, and his conclusion was that I was telling the truth about my experiences at S4.

ROGAN: Do you regret coming forward?

LAZAR: Sometimes. It destroyed my career, my reputation in mainstream science. I was raided by the FBI in 2017 — they came to my business, United Nuclear, looking for Element 115. I had retained a small sample when I left S4. That raid confirmed to me that the government takes my claims seriously, even if publicly they deny everything.

ROGAN: The FBI raid — that was real? They actually came looking for Element 115?

LAZAR: They did. November 2017. They showed up at United Nuclear Scientific Equipment & Supplies, my company in Laingsburg, Michigan. They went through everything. Jeremy documented the aftermath.

ROGAN: What were the test flights like? You said you witnessed the craft in operation.

LAZAR: The craft would lift off vertically with no visible exhaust, no noise — or rather, a faint hissing sound. It would tilt on its side to travel laterally. The movement was unlike anything conventional. No inertia effects — it could go from stationary to high speed instantaneously and stop the same way. On the Wednesday evening flights I witnessed, the craft would emerge from the mountains near Papoose Lake and perform maneuvers for what seemed like a test protocol.

ROGAN: Were there other people working on the project?

LAZAR: I was told there were other scientists, but I had limited contact. The compartmentalization was extreme. I was given access only to the propulsion system. I never met the other scientists working on different aspects. I was assigned to one of nine craft. Each craft was slightly different in design.
